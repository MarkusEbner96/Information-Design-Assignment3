Three speeds of the electric turn

Vienna's car fleet is going electric — but not everywhere at the same pace. This story follows three channels of change: the cars already on the road, the cars being bought new, and the cars changing hands second-hand.

Section 1 of 3 · The slow speed

Vienna's car fleet: still mostly fossil

Since 2011, the overall composition of registered vehicles has shifted — but slowly. Petrol and diesel still dominate the streets. Electric vehicles are growing, but from a tiny base.

Why so slow?
Cars stay on the road for 15–20 years. Even fast growth in new registrations takes decades to fully replace the existing stock — this is the "fleet inertia" problem.

Section 2 of 3 · The fast speed

New registrations: a dramatic shift since 2020

Factory-new vehicles entering the fleet tell a very different story. The share of electric and hybrid drives among new registrations has surged — this is where policy incentives and manufacturer supply are most visible.

2020 turning point
Federal purchase subsidies and COVID recovery packages coincided with a sharp jump in the electric share of new registrations.

Section 3 of 3 · The social speed

Used cars: when does the transition reach everyone?

Most private households buy used cars, not new ones. The used-car market is therefore the clearest indicator of when the e-mobility transition becomes truly accessible — beyond companies and high-income early adopters.

The lag effect
A car registered new in 2021 typically enters the used market years later. The used-EV boom is still arriving — this chart shows its early, still-modest signals.

Takeaways

What this means for Vienna's transition

🐢 The fleet moves slowly

Long vehicle lifespans mean the total stock changes gradually, even when new-car buying habits shift fast.

⚡ New registrations move fast

Subsidies and supply growth have pushed electric vehicles to a meaningful share of new registrations since 2020.

👥 Access still lags

The used-car market — the entry point for most households — shows the transition has not yet reached everyone.

Data & methodology
